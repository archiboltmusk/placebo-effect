Sushruta's Mandate

If Charaka's verdict closed the loophole on what counts as medicine, Sushruta's mandate closes the loophole on who is allowed to hold the scalpel — and by his own standard, the answer has never included a weekend workshop.

Ch. 09

Ch.8 read the first pillar of the Brihatrayi — the Charaka Samhita — against the pharmacological half of the placebo economy: the P&P exemptions, the supplement-as-drug sleight of hand Ch.1 documented. The second pillar, the Sushruta Samhita, is aimed at surgery — and at the specific administrative mechanism Ch.4 called the Mixopathy Crisis: the 2020 Gazette notification authorizing alternative-medicine graduates to perform 58 major surgical procedures after a short postgraduate module.

Acharya Sushruta is routinely invoked to justify that notification — the "father of surgery," reclaiming his rightful place in a modern operating theater. Read the text he actually wrote, and the invocation collapses. The Sushruta Samhita doesn't bless a shortcut to the scalpel. It is, line by line, an argument against one.

This chapter is not an attack on ancient Indian surgery. It is an argument that a state notification calling a certificate course "surgical training" is the exact failure mode the Sushruta Samhita was written to engineer out of existence.

1. The Mandate for Absolute Anatomical Dissection (Sharireshana)

Modern short-term surgical modules for alternative tracks lean almost entirely on textual theory and simulated lectures — bypassing the years of cadaveric dissection and supervised operating-theater time Ch.3 already showed a residency-trained MS surgeon accumulates before touching a patient unsupervised. The Sushruta Samhita (Sharirasthana, Chapter 5) treats this substitution as disqualifying on its face:

Sushruta Samhita, Sharirasthana 5

"Tasmād gatoravam āvaśyam... śruta-pratyakṣataḥ" — therefore, anyone who wishes to acquire a definitive, unshakeable knowledge of surgery must thoroughly examine the structural anatomy of the dead body by direct visual observation, dissecting every single layer.

Sushruta is explicit that Shastra (textual mastery) and Pratyaksha (direct, hands-on experiential mastery) are both mandatory, not substitutable — a practitioner with only the first is, in his framing, simply incompetent. The 2020 notification Ch.4 examined authorizes 58 procedures on the strength of the first alone. By Sushruta's own threshold, that isn't an abbreviated version of surgical training. It's the half of it he singled out as worthless without the other.

2. The Danger of Contentment with Shifting Standards (Alpa-Shruta)

Sushruta anticipated the specific failure mode of a graduate who has mastered a fragment of a discipline and is then authorized to perform the whole of it. In Sutrasthana, Chapter 3, he names this practitioner directly:

Sushruta Samhita, Sutrasthana 3

A practitioner who has only mastered a single aspect of the science, or whose training is shallow and incomplete, is like a one-winged bird that cannot fly. Such a person, when confronted with a complex clinical crisis or an anatomical variation mid-procedure, becomes utterly confused and brings immediate destruction to the patient. The state must actively guard against those who possess superficial knowledge but demand the right to execute deep invasive therapies.

"The state must actively guard against" is Sushruta's own language — not a regulatory metaphor borrowed for this report. The Alpa-Shruta he describes isn't hypothetical: it's the structural product of routing the NEET-rejected graduates Ch.3 mapped through a short surgical module and into the rural operating rooms Ch.2 showed residency-trained MDs refuse to staff. The "one-winged bird" doesn't fail in a classroom. It fails mid-procedure, on a patient who has no way of knowing which kind of practitioner is holding the scalpel.

3. The Rejection of Pure Textual Dogma (Tantra-Yukti)

The cultural-nationalism defense Ch.5 dissected has a surgical variant: that the Sushruta Samhita's descriptions are static, sacred, and therefore beyond revision or testing — that "ancient surgery" is a fixed inheritance rather than a discipline. Sushruta rejects this framing in his own text. In Uttaratantra, Chapter 65, he sets out Tantra-Yukti — a systematic methodology for rational interpretation, refinement, and testing of medical knowledge against real-world outcomes.

Sushruta treats surgery as an expanding, falsifiable science: tools are refined, materials are updated, and protocols that fail to survive clinical endpoints are abandoned. He did not write the Sushruta Samhita as a cultural shield to be invoked unread. He wrote it as the starting point of a discipline he expected to keep changing — which makes "this text justifies freezing surgical standards at a certificate-course level" not a conservative reading of Sushruta, but the one reading his own methodology explicitly forecloses.

4. The Surgical Ethics Blueprint: The Concept of Yogya

In Sutrasthana, Chapter 9 (Yogya-Sutriya), Sushruta lays out a precise preclinical curriculum — centuries before suture pads or surgical simulators — built entirely on non-human models, to be mastered before a student is permitted near a living patient:

Sushruta's Yogya Matrix — Preclinical Simulation
Incisions ↳ practiced on the skins of dead animals or water-filled bags
Extractions ↳ practiced on fruits with dense seeds — jackfruit, gourd
Venepuncture ↳ practiced on the veins of dead animals or lotus stalks
The Living Patient ↳ permitted only after mechanical mastery is demonstrated

The sequencing is the point. Yogya isn't an enrichment exercise layered on top of "real" training — in Sushruta's structure, it is the gate a student must clear before a living patient is even in the room. A regulatory framework that authorizes surgical privileges on the strength of a short workshop hasn't built a faster version of this gate. It has removed it.

So when a state notification calls itself a revival of Sushruta's legacy, the textual record says the opposite. Sushruta demanded exhaustive, layer-by-layer cadaveric dissection joined to hands-on mastery (Sharireshana), warned in his own words that the state must guard against the "one-winged bird" produced by shallow training (Alpa-Shruta), built his text as a living methodology rather than a frozen relic (Tantra-Yukti), and required demonstrated mechanical competence on non-human models before any living patient (Yogya). None of those four conditions is satisfied by a certificate module. This report does not attack ancient Indian surgery — it defends Sushruta's standard against a state that invokes his name while declining to meet it.
