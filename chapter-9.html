01

The "Bond-Age" System

State-sanctioned indentured labour

An engineering or management graduate enters the open market the day they graduate. The allopathic medical graduate walks into a different arrangement entirely: the compulsory service bond.

The debt trap

Young MBBS and postgraduate residents are reported to sign coercive tripartite agreements with banks and government. Refuse a multi-year, poorly managed rural posting, and the penalty is framed as crushing.

The academic interruption. These bond periods are timed to collide with the most critical academic window of a young doctor's life — the formative years needed to prepare for super-specialty exams.

The infrastructure lie. Specialists are dropped into Primary Health Centres without running water, electricity, diagnostic labs, or essential drugs. The doctor cannot practise medicine. They function instead as a bureaucratic shield, absorbing a community's anger over the state's own failure to build.

The financial irony. The people carrying tertiary healthcare on their backs are paid least at the moment their load peaks — pushed into deep financial vulnerability precisely when the system extracts the most from them.

03

Violence as a release valve

Zero workplace security

When a patient dies in an underfunded ICU — no oxygen, no working ventilator, no emergency drugs — the state faces no accountability. The grieving family's anger has nowhere to land except on the nearest visible representative of the system.

The scapegoat dynamic

That representative is the sleep-deprived junior resident. Assaults, vandalism inside emergency wards, and mob violence are described as routine operational hazards.

The institutional gaslighting. When doctors strike for basic measures — working CCTV, restricted entry, armed security — administrations are reported to answer with suspension threats, hostel eviction, and the invocation of essential-services acts. The demand is total, silent submission under lethal conditions.

04

Brain drain is a survival strategy

Flipping the patriotism narrative

The press frames the exodus as a lack of patriotism. The manuscript's argument inverts it: leaving is not betrayal — it is a logical, life-saving migration.

The official framing

"Our brightest abandon the nation."

Departure to the US, UK or Gulf the moment a degree clears is read as disloyalty — a moral failing of the individual doctor.

The structural reading

A refusal to be extorted.

They leave to escape coercive bonds, 80-hour weeks without sleep, and assault on the hospital floor — while wellness cartels and unqualified placeholders draw state protection.

The manifesto verdict
The placeholder economy survives by cannibalising its best minds. The state uses coercive bonds and early-career debt to trap genuine graduates in broken clinics, while forcing tertiary residents into brutal, sleepless shifts under constant threat of violence. If you want a scientifically sound nation, stop treating your life-savers like indentured state property.
